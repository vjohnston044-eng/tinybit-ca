Seven days in: what's working

The reintroduction of salmon and eggs wasn't dramatic. But the energy shift on day 4 was real. Here's what I've noticed and what surprised me.

On day 2, I felt nervous about eating the salmon. After a decade of plant-based eating, fish was... foreign. The smell was stronger than I expected. But that first bite? Rich, satisfying, and my body seemed to recognize it immediately. By day 4, my afternoon energy crash was gone. Not just better, actually gone.

The eggs came on day 3. Pasture-raised, omega-3 rich. I had two for breakfast and felt full until 1pm. That hasn't happened in years. My energy was stable. My mood was stable. No crash.

What surprised me most: the cravings stopped. The constant low-level desire for sweets, snacks, something else. Gone. Just like that. I didn't expect that to be the difference.

The Greek yogurt joined on day 5. Same story. Satiety. Energy. Clarity.

I'm documenting everything in my Vitality Log. The goal isn't perfection. The goal is data. Real information about what my body actually needs, not what I thought it needed.

The plan Food & Fasting

Why I'm rebuilding after veganism

A decade of plant-based eating left me depleted. The nutrients I missed, the energy I lost, and what I'm doing differently this time with real science behind it.

I didn't start vegan for health reasons. I started for ethical reasons, and I was committed. But over ten years, something shifted. My energy tanked. My hair thinned. My motivation disappeared. I blamed stress, menopause, burnout. It wasn't any of those things alone.

What I didn't do right: I didn't supplement strategically. B12, I kind of ignored. Iron absorption without vitamin C, zinc ratios, amino acid completeness. I ate well (lots of vegetables, legumes, whole grains) but not *completely*. And in menopause, incompleteness becomes a problem.

The reintroduction isn't about abandoning ethics. It's about being honest about my body's needs. Wild-caught salmon. Pasture-raised eggs. Greek yogurt. These aren't the only options, but they're the ones I'm starting with because the bioavailability is clear and the sourcing matters to me.

This time, I'm tracking everything. Supplement stack dialed in. Meal logging. Real data instead of assumptions.

Foundation Menopause & Science

Menopause isn't one thing

It's a metabolic shift that affects everything: hunger, sleep, body composition, mood. Here's what the research actually says and what that means for eating and energy.

Menopause isn't a deficiency you fix with one supplement. It's a metabolic recalibration. Estrogen and progesterone drop, which impacts mitochondrial function. Your cells generate energy differently. Your body composition changes. What worked at 40 doesn't work at 50.

The metabolic shift: Your resting metabolic rate drops 2-8% per decade after 30, and menopause accelerates this. Your insulin sensitivity changes. Your hunger hormones change. You're literally a different metabolic system.

Nutrient absorption: Lower estrogen means lower stomach acid, which means lower iron, B12, and calcium absorption. Plant-based sources of these are already less bioavailable. In menopause, this becomes critical.

Protein needs increase: Not because of fitness goals, but because you lose muscle mass faster and your body needs more amino acids for hormonal synthesis. The research is clear on this.

What this means for me: I can't eat the same foods in the same quantities and expect the same results. My body is asking for something different. This isn't weakness or failure. It's biology.
